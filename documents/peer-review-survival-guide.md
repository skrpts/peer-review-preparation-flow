## Peer Review Survival Guide

### Understanding the Process

#### Timeline Expectations
- **Desk decision:** 1-4 weeks. The editor decides whether to send the manuscript for review or desk-reject it. Desk rejection rates at top journals can exceed 50%.
- **Peer review:** 4-12 weeks. Reviewers assess the manuscript. Two to three reviewers is standard. Some journals use single-blind review (reviewers know who you are), others use double-blind (neither party knows), and a growing number offer open review.
- **Decision:** The editor synthesises reviewer recommendations and decides: accept, minor revision, major revision, revise and resubmit, or reject.
- **Revision:** 4-12 weeks to prepare a revision (journals typically set a deadline). Multiple rounds of revision are common for major revisions.
- **Production:** 2-8 weeks from acceptance to online publication. Print publication may take longer.

Total time from submission to publication: typically 6-18 months. Plan accordingly.

#### Decision Types

| Decision | What It Means | What to Do |
|----------|--------------|------------|
| Accept | Rare on first submission. The manuscript is accepted as-is or with minor copyediting. | Celebrate. Respond promptly to production queries. |
| Minor revision | Reviewers had small concerns. Changes required are modest. | Address every comment. Resubmission usually goes back to the editor only, not back to reviewers. |
| Major revision | Significant concerns that require substantial changes. The editor believes the manuscript has potential but needs work. | Take this seriously — it is a positive signal. Address every comment thoroughly. Expect the revised manuscript to go back to reviewers. |
| Revise and resubmit | Similar to major revision but often implies the revised manuscript will be treated as a new submission. | Address all concerns. Be prepared for a new round of review with potentially different reviewers. |
| Reject | The manuscript is not suitable for this journal. | Read the reviews carefully. If the feedback is constructive, revise and submit elsewhere. Do not resubmit to the same journal unless the editor explicitly invites it. |

### Before Submission

#### Pre-Submission Checklist
1. Have at least one colleague outside your immediate team read the manuscript
2. Run the manuscript readiness checker in this skrpt
3. Perform a thorough self-critique using the self-critique generator
4. Select 2-3 target journals using the journal selector — have a fallback plan
5. Prepare all supplementary materials, data availability statements, and compliance declarations
6. Write the cover letter tailored to the specific journal
7. Check formatting against the target journal's author guidelines — journals desk-reject for formatting non-compliance more often than you would expect
8. Upload the manuscript and verify the PDF proof before final submission

#### Suggesting Reviewers
Many journals ask for suggested reviewers. Choose researchers who:
- Have published on the specific topic (but are not your collaborators)
- Are at a similar or higher career stage
- Are from different institutions and ideally different countries
- Would be genuinely appropriate reviewers — editors check for conflicts of interest

You may also be asked for excluded reviewers. This is appropriate if there is a genuine conflict (competing lab, known personal animosity, recent collaboration).

### During Review

The waiting period is anxiety-inducing but unproductive to obsess over. While you wait:
- Start working on the next project or paper
- Do not contact the editor to ask about progress unless the journal's stated timeline has been significantly exceeded (typically 3+ months)
- If you need to contact the editor (e.g., to report a correction in the submitted manuscript), do so briefly and professionally

### Handling Reviewer Feedback

#### Reading Reviews
1. Read all reviews through once without taking action. Your first emotional reaction is rarely your best response.
2. Wait 24-48 hours before drafting responses. This cooling-off period is essential, especially if the reviews feel unfair.
3. Re-read with a pen: categorise each comment as methodological, theoretical, writing, or editorial.
4. Identify the core concerns versus peripheral suggestions. Editors care most about whether the core concerns are addressed.

#### Types of Reviewer Comments

**Constructive criticism you agree with:**
The easiest to handle. Thank the reviewer, make the change, reference the specific location in the revised manuscript. These comments improve your paper — they are a gift, even when they create work.

**Constructive criticism you disagree with:**
The most challenging. You must decide: is the reviewer right and you are emotionally attached to your original approach, or is the reviewer genuinely wrong? If you disagree, provide a substantive, evidence-based justification. Never simply assert "we disagree." Cite published evidence, methodological guidelines, or logical reasoning.

**Requests for additional analyses:**
Evaluate whether the requested analysis would genuinely strengthen the paper. If yes, do it. If no, explain why the current analysis is sufficient and why the requested alternative would not change the conclusions (or would be inappropriate for the data).

**Comments based on misunderstanding:**
These indicate a writing clarity problem, even if the reviewer is "wrong." Revise the relevant text to be clearer. In your response, gently clarify without being condescending.

**Hostile or unprofessional comments:**
Rare but it happens. Maintain professionalism. Address the substance of the comment and ignore the tone. If the hostility is extreme, you may note it to the editor.

### After Revision

#### Resubmission Checklist
1. Every reviewer comment has been addressed — even minor ones
2. Your response letter is complete with page/line references for all changes
3. The revised manuscript is consistent — changes in one section have been reflected throughout
4. Track changes or a highlighted version is provided (if required by the journal)
5. The cover letter to the editor summarises the major changes
6. New references added during revision are correctly formatted
7. Word count still meets the journal's limits
8. All co-authors have approved the revised version

#### If Rejected After Revision
This is painful but not uncommon. Before submitting elsewhere:
1. Incorporate all useful feedback from the reviews
2. Consider whether the rejection indicates a fundamental issue or a poor journal fit
3. Reformat for the next target journal — do not submit in the previous journal's format
4. In the new cover letter, you may mention that the manuscript has been revised based on peer review feedback, but do not name the previous journal

### Career-Stage Considerations

**Early-career researchers:**
- Prioritise getting published over impact factor. A published paper in a solid journal is infinitely more valuable than a desk rejection from Nature.
- Ask your supervisor or a mentor to review your response letter before resubmission
- Keep records of all submission dates, decisions, and revision timelines for your CV

**Mid-career researchers:**
- Consider the strategic value of each publication venue
- Balance high-impact submissions with reliable outlets
- Use revision rounds to genuinely improve the work, not just to satisfy reviewers

**Senior researchers:**
- Model good practice for your team — respond to reviews promptly and professionally
- Support junior co-authors through the revision process
- Consider offering to review for journals where you publish — it is part of the scholarly ecosystem
